Overview and Learning Objectives

For one day per week (Tuesdays), we will focus on visualization research. We will read research papers on some topic/theme, students will make presentations about the papers, and we will discuss. One goal is to try to develop ideas for new research projects. On the other day per week (Thursdays), we will focus on visualization system/application development. Students will learn how to develop interactive visualization tools using languages and libraries like Vega-Lite, D3, and JavaScript.

Textbooks

There are no required textbook for this course. However, there are a collection of resources that you should find helpful for assignments throughout the course. There are also readings that we will cover throughout the course which are all available while on the GT VPN (or on campus).

Course Format

The course will consist mainly of two parts: reading and discussing research papers about information visualization, and learning how to program web-based visualizations (predominantly using d3.js).

    Reading Presentation

    This is a group assignment where your group will present and lead discussion about the reading for a specific day. You will need to do one of these during the semester. Please use the link below to sign up for the reading for that day.

    Overall length: Aim for roughly 20 minutes of total "content" (not counting discussion that will happen after). The discussion (including that coordinated by the Q&A coordinator) should be another 20-30 minute based on class engagement. Plan accordingly to make sure you have a good balance among the 4 roles/parts below.

    Roles: Each group member will have specific roles to ensure that everyone contributes and participates. These roles are to guide who presents what during the presentation, but all group members should read the paper and contribute to the overall presentation for that day. All group members need to be present the day they present.

    • Motivation Presenter: This person will present a brief overview of the paper.
      • Intro
        • What is the motivation for the paper?
        • What is the problem it is trying to solve?
        • What is the goal?
        • What are the contributions?
      • What does the paper do?
        • e.g., if it was a study, what did it study? If it is a visualization tool, what does it do?
    • Example/Demo/Video/Result Presenter: This person presents an example or highlights the results of the paper
      • For a study, this could include a highlight of the results, example of findings, summaries of findings, etc.
      • When videos are available, subsets of the videos can be used.
      • When demos are available, showcasing the demo is good here.
    • Reflection/Future Work Presenter: This person presents an overview of the discussion/future work of the presented work (and potentially how it ties in to other readings this semester)
      • What does the paper say in terms of broader discussion or future work?
      • Are there higher-level topics the paper reflects on?
    • Q&A Coordinator: This person will present a set of discussion questions for the class and generally lead discussion.
      • Questions could be about parts of the paper that seem "odd" or "limitations", potential applications to other areas/domains, impact, missing opportunities, etc.
      • Try not to make these generic, like "what did you all think about the study?", but rather something more specific to the reading, such as "condition 2 had participants use only the second version of the visualization, do you all think that created a confound?

    What to submit: The 4 roles above should coordinate and create a single presentation slide deck of some sort. Feel free to use any online slide software (Google, PowerPoint, etc.) to make you slides. When you are done, download a .pptx of your presentation to this assignment. Each team member must submit the file.

    This individual assignment will have you think and reflect critically about one of the readings this semester and produce a reading reflection document.

    Reflection document requirements:
    • 2-3 pages. Single spaced, single column, reasonable font (~12pt)
    • Don't just repeat the paper. Yes, most introduction sections of papers do a pretty good job giving a summary (or at least, they should!), but this assignment should be more about a reflection on (and related to) the reading for that day.
    • Consider the pattern of summary -> interpret -> reflect for the key points of the paper. That means that you should identify one or more key points made in the paper, interpret what these mean, and then reflect more broadly on them.
    • Useful to end your reflection document with open ended research questions, open discussion, etc.

    What to submit: Submit a pdf of your reflection document

    Due: This assignment is due before the end of the semester, but you can select any reading throughout the semester and submit your reflection anytime before the end of the semester.

    Research Proposal

    This assignment will pull together content and skills you have developed over the course of the semester. This includes the topics and content of the reading and discussion during class, as well as the ability to articulate clear visualization research goals, questions, methods, etc. For this individual assignment, you will write a proposal for a potential new research project in the area of information visualization regarding one or more of the topics we covered this semester.

    A good place to start for this is to think about which "type" of research (and thus proposal) you want to focus on.

    • New visualization tool: Did one of the areas we cover reveal a gap for a new vis? New interaction technique? Write up what this gap is and how your proposed work will cover this gap. Make sure you discuss your design goals (and perhaps a few sketches of the visualization) to help describe what you're proposing.
    • New study: Is there a gap in what we know about visualization in a specific area/topic? Does it need a well-designed study to understand it better? Great! Write up what this gap is, what the research questions are, and what you methodology is for answering it.
    • Survey/Taxonomy: Is there a topic we covered that is missing a survey or taxonomy of visualizations in that area? This sometimes happens with really new topics. Ok, write up a proposal about how you are going to go about this. What is your method for collecting the papers, how will you code/categorize them, etc?
    • There are other "types", but these above should get your started

    Since good research builds on prior work, it is recommended that you cite at least 5 references as part of your proposal to situate your work (and likely define whatever gap/need you outlined).

    Ideally, the result of this is something that you could hand to a professor in a following semester to do a independent study, or maybe even a PhD in vis

    In terms of evaluation, we will grade your proposal based on clarity, thoughtfulness, and how well it is articulated. Does the proposal explain how the proposed work builds on existing work (and how)? Does the methodology of what you propose match the need that you articulated? Is it scoped as something that would be accomplished in 1-2 semesters? We are looking for proposals that are not too simple, but also not way to complex in scope.

    What to turn in: 2-4 page whitepaper proposing your research. Submit the pdf.
